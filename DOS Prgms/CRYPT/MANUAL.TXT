 COMit is communications software.  A modem connects your computer to the
 telephone line.  Communications software controls data flow to and from the
 modem and manages your keyboard, display, and disk files while using the
 modem.

 At the nucleus of COMit is the dialing directory, an easy to use database
 where you can store names, phone numbers, and modem setups.  On your screen it
 appears as a wire-bound notebook, which you can "flip" through, search, or
 sort into alphabetical order.  You can even add pages with your own notes,
 instructions, and records!

 Move to any entry, press a key, and COMit instructs the modem to dial for you,
 and to redial if the line is busy.  Once connected, your computer acts as a
 terminal, giving you access to the remote computer's resources.  Seven
 built-in transfer protocols provide compatibility with nearly any other system
 for sending and receiving disk files.

 You can record your session to a printer or disk file, or when something has
 slipped out of view, you can use COMit's "scroll-back" feature to review as
 many lines as you want.

 With a simple but powerful "scripting" capability, you can prepare responses
 to a remote computer's prompts before a call, so that routine information
 requests and file transfers can be done automatically and completely
 unattended.  You needn't be a programmer to prepare these scripts.  COMit, by
 "watching" your last call, can write a script for you, and it is stored right
 in the dialing directory so you don't have dozens of files to manage!

 Do you want your computer to answer calls from others?  It's easy to do with
 COMit's host mode.  Callers can view, send, and receive files, and you,
 remotely or locally, can set 3 levels of password security, authorizing access
 to just the callers and disk files you wish.  A "chat" feature is also
 available for typing messages back and forth while online.

 COMit goes beyond other communications software by adding Class 2, 4, 5, and
 7 MNP (and in certain editions, V.42bis) to modems which may not have the
 capability built into the hardware.  MNP and/or V.42bis ensures flawless
 transmissions, even when phone connection quality is poor, and optional
 compression of data "on the fly" for sending and receiving potentially more
 than three times the rated speed of the modem.

 To aid in original setup as well as troubleshooting, COMit provides an
 extensive array of diagnostic features.  It can locate your modem, find
 conflicting devices, and in many cases, it can even compensate for incorrect
 switch settings.  You can also quickly view your modem's S-Register settings
 and their meanings.   For troubleshooting that goes beyond communications,
 COMit provides CPU, DOS version, math coprocessor, and video card
 identification, a RAM test, a speed and performance test, and listings of

 Chapter 1   Learning & Installing COMit
 ---------------------------------------
 This manual gives you a "hard copy" reference for installing and using COMit.

 You may prefer (and we highly recommend) getting familiar with COMit first by
 using the "manual on a disk".  It goes into great detail for communications
 and data storage terminology, as well as all the features of COMit.  The
 built-in glossary makes it especially useful for first-time modem users.

 To do so, you must first go through the installation process.

 Installing on a Hard Disk
 -------------------------
 1. Put your COMit disk in a drive and close its latch.

 Normally, you'll use Drive A.

 2. Now change the drive letter to match the drive you've put the COMit disk
 into.  This is done by entering the drive letter and a colon.  For example:

    A: [Enter]

 Next, simply type:

   INSTALL [Enter].

 You'll be prompted by the program for the remaining steps.

 The usual process includes making a subdirectory to hold COMit's files,
 unpacking those files that have been compressed to save disk space,
 and copying them to the destination you indicate.

 After installation, you'll be taken to the drive and directory where
 COMit has been installed.

 To start COMit, with the "help" manual available, simply type:

   COMITH [Enter].

 Or, to browse though the help manual outside of COMit, simply type "HELP".

 Chapter 3 gives other ways to start COMit.

 Bits Column
 -----------
 The bits specification is a digit (for data bits) a letter (for parity), and a
 digit (for stop bits.)  The common settings: 8N1, 7E1, and 7O1 can be selected
 by pressing the space bar.  Custom settings can be entered by pressing [F1].

 Data bits are the bits that represent the characters transmitted.  COMit
 allows setting 5,6,7, or 8 data bits.  In most cases you'll want 8 data bits,
 but some information services require 7 data bits.

 Parity refers to a type of error checking that is used in communications.
 Comit supports all 5 types.

   N None
   O Odd
   E Even
   M Mark
   S Space

 Your data bits and parity setting must agree with that of the computer you are
 calling.

 Stop bits are sent immediately after the data and parity bits.  The usual
 setting is 1, but sometimes 2 stop bits are used to slow down the transmission
 just a bit so that a slower receiving device can keep up.

 Rate Column
 -----------
 Rate refers to the speed at which data is transmitted and received, measured
 as bps (bits per second.)

 COMit supports the following bps rates:

  50, 75, 110, 134, 150, 300, 600, 1200, 1800, 2000, 2400, 4800, 9600,
  19200, 38400, 57600, 76800, 115200

 For most communications you'll probably be using 300, 1200, 2400, 9600,
 19200, 38400, or 57600.  These can be selected by pressing [Spacebar].
 Other rates can be entered by pressing [F1].

 The rate cannot exceed the capabilities of your modem and/or serial card,
 but it may (on some modems) exceed the maximum transmission rate over the
 phone lines.  For example, you may be able to set the rate to 38400 for
 a 9600 bps modem.  38400, in this case, is the rate between modem and
 computer. If your modem doesn't allow this, or if you are using COMit's
 (software) MNP or V.42bis, the rate must agree with the rate (or one of
 the rates) expected by the computer to be called.

 MNP Column
 ----------
 MNP stands for Microcom Networking Protocol.  It provides for error-free
 communication at (potentially) much greater speeds through data compression.
 COMit can implement MNP (in software) for modems which don't have it built
 into the hardware (or where the modem's MNP has been turned off by a special
 initialization string.)

 As provided with certain modems, COMit interfaces to a driver file, V42.DRV,
 that implements V.42 error prevention and data compression in software.
 (Other modems may have it built entirely into the hardware, making the driver
 file and any special procedures unnecessary.)  Software V.42 is available if
 if "V.42bis" displays above the copyright notice on your dialing directory
 screen, and if "+H1" is somewhere in your initialization string.  For purposes
 of discussion, we treat V.42 and V.42bis as if they were two more classes
 of MNP.

 The "classes" of MNP supported by COMit are...
 NONE   - Regular communications (or MNP implemented in the modem's hardware.)
 MNP2   - Error correction only
 MNP4   - Error correction with improved speed due to bigger data packets.
 MNP5   - Error correction + basic data compression
 MNP7   - Error correction + enhanced data compression.
 V42    - Error correction only (With certain modems.)
 V42bis - Error correction + much enhanced data compression. (Certain modems.)
 REQD   - MNP (or V.42) required.

 Term Column
 -----------
 A terminal is a keyboard and video display with very little or no computing
 power of its own, connected to a mainframe or minicomputer.

 Each type of terminal has its own codes that tell it how and where on the
 screen to display data, and special keys that send codes to the the remote
 computer.  Terminal Emulation makes your personal computer act like a specific
 type of terminal.  COMit supports the most important emulations:

   TTY   (Teletype)
   ANSI  (American National Standards)

   VT100 (Digital Equipment Corporation)

 the host computer's control.

 Chapter 7  - Calling with COMit
 ===============================

 To make a call with COMit, go to the Dialing Directory Screen, locate the name
 you want, and press [Enter].

 Or, from COMit's main menu, select option /OD.  The currently indicated name
 will be dialed.

 Here's what happens...

 1. COMit checks the parameters for the dialing directory entry. If any of them
 are invalid, the Invalid Dialing Attempt screen is displayed, where the
 problem is identified for you, and you can make the indicated correction(s).

 2. Next, COMit displays the Online Screen, which is blank except the status
 line at the bottom.  The Initialization String is sent to the modem.  (You'll
 see it and the modem's response at the top of the screen.)  COMit waits for
 the modem to respond with "OK".  (Two attempts are made, if necessary.)  If no
 "OK" is received, the Failed to Connect! Menu displays.  (Failure to get
 the "OK" may indicate a rate too fast for the modem, an invalid initialization
 string for the modem, an incorrect COM port or IRQ, or a modem that needs to
 be reset manually (by powering down.)

 3. If a script has been defined for the dialing directory entry, COMit
 executes it up to the first "W", "O", "D", "S", or "R" command it contains.
 (This allows any pre-call inputs and messages within the script to be
 processed.)  If you press [Esc] at any of the script's prompts, the call is
 canceled.  If you press [Alt-S], the script is ignored, and execution
 continues as if it were a non-scripted call.

 4. COMit dials the number and waits for the modem to respond with a CONNECT
 message.  While COMit is dialing, the name, the script message (if
 applicable), and the dialing command are displayed.

 Time and Terminal Status
 ------------------------
 This option is available by selecting [T] from COMit's online menu or by
 pressing [Alt-T] while online.  The duration of the call thus far, and the
 time taken for the last file transfer is displayed.

 If special key mappings are in effect (for VT100 terminal emulation) they
 are listed on the screen.  This tells what keys on your PC keyboard are used
 to represent the keys on the VT100 keyboard.

 From the Time and Terminal Status screen, you can also change the functioning
 of the [Enter] key and [Backspace] by pressing 0, 1, 2, or 3.  This may be
 required when communicating with certain host computers.  "1" means [Enter]
 sends a carriage return and line feed (instead of a carriage return only.)
 "2" means the Backspace key sends the DEL character, ASCII 128 (instead of
 ASCII 8.)  "3" means options 1 and 2 are both in effect.  "0" goes back
 to normal.


 Hang Up
 -------
 This option is available by selecting [H] from COMit's online menu or by
 pressing [Alt-H] while online.  COMit terminates the online session and
 returns you to the dialing directory.

 Echo
 ----
 This option is available by selecting [E] from COMit's online menu or by
 pressing [Alt-E] while online.

 The effect is to toggle echoing ON or OFF.  When echo is "Yes", full duplex
 communications are used.  When echo is "No", half duplex is in effect.

 If you can't see your typing, this is usually a clue that echo needs to be
 changed to "Y".  If letters are doubled when you type, echo should be changed
 to "N".  The status line (at the bottom of the screen) shows the current
 setting.


 Note Page
 ---------
 [N] from COMit's online menu or [Alt-N] while online brings up the note page
 for the current dialing directory entry.   You can write notes to yourself
 about the call here.

 The notes are available to you after the call (by pressing [N] when the cursor
 is on the dialing directory entry) and they'll be available during future
 calls.

 Escape from the note page resumes your online session.

 Printing Tagged Files
 ----------------------
 Option P lets you print all the tagged files on your line printer.  A submenu
 lets you say whether or not a form feed is to be sent after each file.  Be
 careful to include only ASCII Files for printing.  The exception is non-ASCII
 files containing graphic images that can be interpreted by the make and model
 of your printer you may have.

 Test-Reading Tagged Files
 -------------------------
 Option T can be very useful.  It causes COMit to go through your tagged files
 list.  Each file is read from beginning to end.  If any disk errors are
 encountered or if a file is not found this is noted for you.  This  prevents
 unexpected problems later, which can waste time and cost you money while
 online.

 As COMit test-reads each file, it computes the file's CRC and reports the
 result.  For example:   OK, CRC -> 28,927.  The number may be from 0 to
 65,535, and is computed by performing a calculation on every byte in the file,
 taking into account the byte's position.   Thus, there's a good chance that
 two files with the same CRC have the same contents.  If there's any doubt
 you've received a file correctly, or you're not sure you have the latest
 version, you and (and a friend with COMit) can compute CRC's on the same file
 before investing the time to resend it.

 For the lines on your tagged files list having wildcard specifications
 (instead of individual file names) the CRC reported is the combined CRC for
 all files matching the specification.

 Check all COM ports
 -------------------
 This option is available by selecting /DC from COMit's Main Menu.

 It is useful when initially setting up COMit (See Chapter 5) or when you are
 having trouble getting a modem to respond.

 "Check all COM ports" tries every COM number and IRQ Number with each normal
 Port Address to determine what modems and serial cards are present on your
 computer.

 The first step for each COM, IRQ, and Address combination is an internal
 loopback test.  If it fails, "No serial card or internal modem found" is
 reported.  If it succeeds, COMit attempts to initialize the modem.  If the
 modem responds with "OK", COMit asks for the Modem's ID and reports it to you.

 The LineStat and ModemStat columns of the display give information that may be
 helpful to technicians.   The numbers can be interpreted from the following
 table, but be aware that for non-existing serial cards or modems, the numbers
 shown may be meaningless.

      Line Status Register              Modem Status Register
      --------------------              ---------------------
      7 Timeout                         7 Data Carrier detect
      6 Transmit shift register empty   6 Ring indicator
      5 Transmit shift hold reg empty   5 Data set ready
      4 Break detected                  4 Clear to send
      3 Framing error                   3 Delta data carrier detect

 Chapter 13  Writing and Using Scripts
 =====================================
 COMit allows you to write miniature programs called "scripts" to automatically
 handle the progress of a call.  Scripts save time and money, and they make it
 easy for inexperienced users to perform routine tasks.

 When you think of it, most online communication sessions consist of waiting
 for prompts from the host computer, and answering them appropriately. A
 script, in its most simple form, waits for specific prompts and automatically
 types the appropriate answers.  Beyond this you can add commands to control
 file sending and receiving, hanging up, displaying local messages to the user
 and getting responses, and jumping to different parts of the script based on
 user requests or conditions that may be present.

 Each dialing directory entry may have one (optional) script attached to it,
 but as you'll see, you can create a "menu" within the script that makes it
 handle all the special tasks that might be anticipated.

 Script Commands
 ----------------
 All COMit script commands are a single letter followed by (optional)
 parameters.  Each command is on a separate line.  Invalid command letters and
 blank lines are ignored.  Your may use upper and/or lower case letters.

 You may add comments to any script command line by adding a semicolon (;) as
 shown in many of the examples below. If a semicolon is required as part of a
 script parameter use ";;"

 The scripting commands are...

 T Timeout         M Message         I If            S Send
 W Waitfor         K KbdInput        G Goto          R Receive
 O Output          P Pause           E Else          H Hangup
 Q Quit            D Dial            L Label


 They are discussed on the following pages, in alphabetical order.  You'll
 probably want to learn them in a more logical order...

 - Start with W and O (the two most important script commands) first.
 - Then look at S, R, H, (the commands for sending files, receiving files, and
   hanging up.)
 - The Q, P, and T commands are good to learn next.
 - Finally, you'll want to learn M, K, D, I, G, E, and L.  These commands
   display messages to the operator, get responses, and do if-then branching
   for very advanced capabilities.

 "D" Dial
 --------
 Normally, you'll put all your "M" (message) and "K" (keyboard input) commands
 at the start of a script.  This allows the local operator to prepare data for
 the call and/or make choices from a menu you've created.  The call is not
 actually dialed until a "W", "O", "S", "R", or "D" command is encountered in
 the script.  The "D" command is provided as a way to make dialing begin before
 it normally would.  For example...

 M We're going to dial...
 D
 M We've dialed now...

 There are no parameters for the dial command.

 "E" Else
 ------
 The "E" (else) command is used with the "I" (if) command.  You'll normally
 have an "I" command, followed by one or more commands that tell what the
 script is to do if the "if" condition is true.  The "E" command signifies the
 end of the things to be done if the condition is true.  Script execution
 resumes at the command following the "E" if the "if" condition is false.  See
 the "I" command for an example.

 You may nest "if and else" commands between other "if and else" commands.

 The "E" command has no parameters.

 "G" Goto(Label)
 -------------
 The "G" (goto) command causes COMit to search the script for a particular
 label (defined by the "L" command.  Execution of the script resumes at the
 command following the label.  For example:

 G GETNAME              ;Go to the GETNAME label.
 M Enter your Name:     ;Display "Enter your name:"
 L GETNAME              ;This is the GETNAME label
 K 5                    ;Get input and store it in memory 5

 In this case "Enter your Name" is never displayed, because the G command
 causes the script to "jump over" it.

 If the "goto" label is not found, the script terminates and the operator can
 take over manually.

 ----------------------
 The hangup command signifies the end of a script.  It causes the phone to be

 Errorlevel is an optional parameter (a number from 0 to 255).  It may be used
 to pass back a result code to an MS-DOS batch file if COMit is being run
 automatically.  See Startup Parameters for more information.

 Chapter 14 - Transfer Protocols
 ===============================


 Sending and receiving files is one of the most important capabilities to be
 handled by a communications program.  To do so, a connection must be made
 between the two computers, preferably with one computer in host mode, and
 the other computer calling it.  Then, to transfer files, one computer
 sends (uploads) and the other is receives (downloads.)  The transfer
 protocol coordinates the process by defining when the transfer starts
 and ends, how errors are sensed and what to do if they occur, whether data
 is passed in blocks or a continuous stream, and how to relay the information
 about file naming, sizes, and dates.  COMit supports the most popular file
 transfer protocols.  In most cases, you'll simply choose the most
 sophisticated protocol available to both computers.


 ASCII Transfer Protocol
 -----------------------
 ASCII is the most primitive (in fact, most experts wouldn't consider it a
 protocol at all) but it is the most universally available. No error
 detection is employed (unless you're using MNP.)  The end of file is signified
 by a Ctrl-Z character (ASCII 26).  Because binary files might have Ctrl-Z's
 and other control characters anywhere, they can't be sent reliably with ASCII.

 Unless the data to be sent is pure text, don't use ASCII. Even for text files,
 use ASCII transfers only when no other protocol is available.

 XMODEM
 -------
 In XMODEM transfers, a file is sent in blocks of 132 bytes.  The transfer is
 started by the receiver, which sends a NAK character (ASCII 21) at 10-second
 intervals until the sender responds with the first block.  Validity checking
 is done by evaluating the checksum and the other non-data bytes in the block.

 The receiver expects no more than a 1 second interval between bytes.  If
 an error is detected, the receiver sends a NAK and the block may be resent up





 ÚÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄ¿
 ³COMit Operating Manual    Copyright (C) 1992 Tradewind Software   Page 67   ³
 ÀÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÙ

 to 10 times.  Otherwise, an ACK (ASCII 6) is sent to acknowledge correct
 receipt.  An EOT character (ASCII 4) instead of another block indicates end of
 file, and the receiver responds with a final ACK.  The Ctrl-X character (ASCII
 24) between blocks by sender or receiver requests cancellation of the
 transfer.

   ÚÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄ¿

   ÃÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄÄ´

 XMODEM-CRC
 -----------
 The XMODEM-CRC protocol improves on XMODEM by using CRC instead of a checksum
 for validity checking.  This provides a much better chance that errors due to
 a bad phone line will be caught.  Instead of the initial NAK character(s), the
 receiving computer sends "C" until the first block is received.  (After
 sending 3 C's without a response, the receiver assumes the sender is unable to
 handle CRC, so it sends NAK characters, and regular XMODEM is attempted.)

 YMODEM
 ------
 The YMODEM protocol, sometimes called XMODEM-1K, is like XMODEM-CRC, but
 faster transfers can be achieved (when a phone connection is good) because
 1024 bytes of data are sent per block.  The sender may also use 128 byte
 blocks, at its discretion.  Blocks with 1024 bytes have STX (ASCII 2) as the
 start of block character instead of SOH.

 YMODEM-BATCH
 ------------
 One (usually insignificant) problem of XMODEM, XMODEM-CRC, and YMODEM
 transfers is that the file size on the receiving computer is always an even
 multiple of 128 bytes.  (ASCII zeros are padded onto the end.)   YMODEM Batch
 solves this problem and allows more than one file to be to be sent in a
 single transfer by including file name(s) and size(s) along with the data.
 After requesting file(s) from the sender, the receiver only needs to specify
 the drive and path for local storage.

 In batch transfers the initial block number is zero (instead of one) to
 indicate that it contains a file name and size.  After each file, another
 "filename block" is expected.  A filename block with ASCII zeros instead of a
 file name indicates the end of the batch.

 Because file naming is handled automatically, YMODEM-Batch is well suited
 suited for unattended operation in scripts.

 YMODEM-G & YMODEM-G-BATCH
 -------------------------
 These protocols correspond to YMODEM and YMODEM-BATCH but can be much faster
 because the sender does not wait for an ACK character before sending each
 block.  In addition, the timing is relaxed to make it more suitable for use on
 some transmission links where data is sent in bursts, with (sometimes) more
 than one-second delays.

 YMODEM-G and G-BATCH should be used only with MNP, V.42, or V.42bis, where
 an error-free connection is assured, because these "G" protocols abort
 (without resending a block) the instant an error is detected.  If the
 MNP, V.42 or V.42bis is implemented in the modem's hardware, there is still
 the potential for error (and an aborted transfer) because the data path
 between modem and computer is not error-corrected.  The chance of errors is
 reduced if you make sure your modem's initialization string calls for RTS/CTS
 local flow control.  (The "standard" init strings in COMit's setup do this
 where possible.)

 To start a YMODEM-G or G-Batch transfer, the receiver sends "G" instead of "C"
 to say it's ready.  (After 3 G's and no response, C's are sent and regular
 YMODEM or YMODEM-Batch is attempted.)

 One disadvantage of the "G" protocols is that cancellation (using Ctrl-X) is
 sometimes messy.  By the time the sender receives the Ctrl-X, a lot of data
 may already be in transit.  You may may need to ignore several pages of
 unintelligible data on the screen before the sender's menu is redisplayed.

 ZMODEM
 ------
 ZMODEM has many features that make it preferable in many situations, and the
 features are automatically negotiated, based on the capabilities and needs of
 both systems.  Among them are...

  - 32-bit (instead of 16-bit) CRC for better error detection.
  - Ability to pass the file date and time, as well as the name and size.
  - Optional ability to overwrite existing files.
  - Optional crash recovery. This, in essence, is the ability to skip files
    the receiver already has, and to append data to files having the same
    name, date and time, but shorter length.
  - Optional Autodownload. The receiving computer, when it "sees" a specific
    string of characters, knows that the sender is ready to start.  The
    operator, after requesting the files from the host computer, doesn't need
    to take any special steps, such as [PgDn] and selecting the protocol.
  - Ability for the sending computer to tell the receiver how many files and
    bytes remain in the whole batch.  (YMODEM-Batch is only able to report
    bytes remaining in the current file.)

 Rather than block numbers, ZMODEM deals with file positions.  Thus, the
 receiving computer, if it senses an error, can issue a "position request" to
 the sender.  The sender goes back to the requested position and retransmits.
 This same capability allows skipping of data the receiver already has.  In
 crash recovery, the receiver does a position request to its end-of-file, and
 the sender starts there if additional data remains.

 See Terminal Options & Paths (Chapter 5) for how to set the ZMODEM options.

 Chapter 15  Errors & Troubleshooting
 ====================================

 Modem Not Working
 -----------------
 One of the most common problems with modems is having more than one device on
 the same COM port (even if the other device is not in use.)  This may causes
 "hanging up" the phone as soon as a call is dialed or answered, or other
 erratic behavior.

 To troubleshoot this for an internal modem, try running option /DC from
 COMit's Main Menu WITHOUT the modem installed.  If any serial ports or modems
 are listed, you'll need to set your (new) modem to a different COM# and/or
 IRQ, or remove or reconfigure the conflicting devices.

 For an external modem, disconnect it.  Option /DC should show "Serial port
 exists but no modem found" for the COM number to be used.


 Modem Diagnostic Lights
 -----------------------
 If you press [Alt-R] when online or when COMit is intitializing the modem
 or dialing, a set of three "lights" replaces the rate area of the status
 line at the bottom of your screen.  They appear as "RxTxCd".  When any
 of the lights is ON the color changes to bright white on green (or white on
 black, if monochrome.)  When OFF, the color is black on a white background.
 [Alt-R] again, toggles back to rate display.  (For modems that utilize
 the software V.42 driver, this feature is unavailable during a call while the
 driver is active.)

 Rx Light - This light goes ON when data is received from the modem.  When
 COMit is doing disk access, or when the receive buffer is full, COMit tells
 the modem to stop giving it incoming data.  The "Rx" legend is replaced by
 "--" at these times to indicate RTS (request to send) is OFF.

 If the light goes ON (in the RX position) while "--" is displayed, it means
 the modem is violating flow control.  (It is still providing data after
 COMit has told it to stop.)  If COMit senses this during a disk access, "??"
 replaces the "--".  If you see this, you should modify your initialization
 string to ensure RTS/CTS flow control is enabled.

 Tx Light - This light flashes ON when COMit sends data to the modem.  If
 "--" replaces the "Tx" legend, it means the modem has told COMit to stop
 sending by turning CTS (clear to send) OFF.  The dashes at the Tx position
 may also mean the modem (if external) is OFF or not connected.

 Cd Light - This light is ON to indicate carrier detect.  (The local modem
 is sensing the signal from the remote modem.)  Upper case "CD" (instead of
 "Cd") indicates the data set ready signal is also high.  Normally, CD should
 light up soon after a connection is made, and turn off when either modem
 hangs up.  "&C2" is included in most initialization strings to ensure this
 is the case, so that COMit knows when an online session has terminated.

 Modem not connected to phone
 ----------------------------
 This is a problem more often than you might think.

 Check your connections.  Make sure the cable that goes to the wall outlet is
 plugged into the correct jack at the back of the modem.

 Hearing touch tone sounds doesn't necessarily mean the modem is connected.
 (You need to hear a dial tone first!)

 Modem not waiting long enough
 -----------------------------
 COMit, when dialing, waits a certain number of seconds while it watches for
 a "connect" message from the modem.  Depending on the phone system in use,
 the number of rings required for the remote system to answer, and the time
 the two modems require for their initial "handshake", the wait before "giving
 up" may need to be changed.  See Terminal Options & Paths (Chapter 5) for how
 to change the wait time.

 When you change the wait time, COMit automatically changes the time it tells
 the modem to wait.  It does this by sending ATS7= (with the wait time) just
 after your initialization string.  (If your initialization string contains
 an ATS7 request, COMit bypasses this step.)  A countdown indicator (at the
 lower right of the screen) shows how many seconds until COMit will stop
 waiting.

 In case of problems you can time the delay needed by calling the remote system
 (on a voice line) and count how long it takes between the last digit dialed
 and the moment you first hear a whistling sound.  Add 15 seconds or more,
 especially when you are communicating at 9600bps or above and using MNP or
 or V.42, because in these cases, the handshake can take longer.


 Modem disconnects soon after connection established
 ---------------------------------------------------
 This can be because the remote system has rejected the protocol or rate you
 are using. (Perhaps it requires MNP, V.42, or a particular bps rate, which,
 if available on your modem, can be selected by changing the MNP setting or
 initialization string.)   It may also happen if your initialization string is
 requesting hardware MNP (or V.42bis) and you've requested software MNP
 (instead of NONE) in the dialing directory entry or (in host mode) you've
 answered "Y" to "use MNP".

 Another possibility is that the data carrier signal is being dropped,
 briefly or continuously.  This is sometimes handled by changing the
 initialization string to include &C0 and/or &D0, (instead of &C1&D2), or by
 adding "@" as the first character of the phone number or dial prefix.
 (If you do this, you will need to manually hang up each call by pressing
 /H after an on-line session.)

 Wrong number
 ------------
 Consider whether or not a 1 and/or area code is required before the number.
 Try dialing the number on a voice line.  You should hear a "whistle tone"
 when the remote computer answers.

 Problems with MNP
 -----------------
 Whenever using COMit's (software) MNP, the modem's (hardware) MNP (if it
 exists) must be turned off.  This is done by selecting an initialization
 string that does so (usually through an &Q0, \N0 or \N1 command.)  The modem's
 XON/XOFF flow control should also be disabled with a code in the init string,
 if flow control is not disabled by default.

 For software MNP or V.42, the rate to be used should normally be the
 connect rate (not faster.)  "Adjust to caller's rate" under Host Options and
 Paths should be set to "Y".  Modems capable of "speed buffering" are a
 possible exception.  If &Q6 requests "speed buffering" (see your modem manual)
 you can add this to your initialization string and use a higher rate, with
 "adjust to caller's rate" set to "N".

 Conversely, if you want to use the modem's MNP and/or V.42 capabilities,
 your dialing directory entry should say "NONE" in the MNP column, and for
 Host Mode, "N" for "use MNP" in Host Options and Paths.  "Adjust to
 caller's rate" under Host Options and Paths should normally be "N" in this
 case.  Be sure, if you customize the initialization string, that RTS/CTS
 flow control is enabled. (This is done with &K3 on some modems, &R1*LR2*LG2
 on some others.  Consult your modem manual.)


 Problems Starting an Upload or Download
 ---------------------------------------
 The most common cause of a problem is initiating your local procedure
 (by pressing [PgUp], [PgDn], [/][S] or [/R]) at the wrong time.  Wait
 until the host system prompts you to start your download or upload.

 Also, make certain you choose the same protocol locally (on the Protocol
 Selection Menu) as the protocol you requested in response to the host's
 prompts.  Be sure you've selected receive (download) or send (upload)
 on your local menu to correspond with what you told the host you wanted
 to do.

 In case of errors, press [Ctrl-X] several times, or (on some systems,
 [Ctrl-C]) to get the host's attention again.

VT52 Terminal Emulation.
------------------------

COMit provides emulation of most features of Digital Equipment's VT52
VT100 terminals.  Simply request VT52 in the "Term" column of any dialing
directory entry that requires it.  The following actions are supported
for VT52 emulation, in response to escape sequences the host may send:

   Cursor up, down, left,right
   Erase to end of screen, erase to end of line
   Cursor to line n, column m.
   Identify. (Esc/Z)
   Enter, exit alternate keypad mode.
   Enter, exit auto print mode.  (Data is printed and displayed.)
   Enter, exit printer controller mode. (Data is printed, non-display.)

Up arrow, down arrow, left arrow, and right arrow.  F1, F2, F3, and F4 act
as PF1, PF2, PF3, and PF4.

[-], [+] and [.]) correspond to those on the VT52 as closely as possible.
(It may be necessary to press NumLock in some cases.)

Other Considerations in VT52 and VT100 Emulation
-------------------------------------------------
Your definitions for the F-Keys are available by pressing [Alt] plus the
F-key you've defined (under F-Key Setups.)

Up Arrow (for review scrolled lines) is not available.  Instead, use [/][B]
at the online menu to do this.  Depending on the status of the numeric keypad
it may be necessary to use [/][R] or [/][S] instead of [PgUp] and [PgDn] for
uploading and downloading.  (Substitute your Menu Access Key if you've
used the F-Key Setup screen to override the default [/].)

Because the host computer has control over your printer in VT emulations, it
is important to have a printer connected to LPT1 (online and ready with paper)
if any printing is to be done.  COMit displays no message to tell you the host
is attempting to print.  Logging data (Alt-L) works as in non-VT52 and VT100,
emulation, and data sent to the printer is included in the log file.  You may
also use [Alt-C] for printer capture.

Scripting and other features should work normally.  Note that the arrow and
F-keys (when used in scripts) are specified by the escape codes they generate.
For example, VT52 up arrow is shown as the 3-character sequence: "^[A".

VT100 automatic printing functions, and reporting to the host of printer
availability and status are available for the LPT1 printer port only.

If you need to change the codes sent by the [Enter] and [Backspace] keys,
you can press [Alt-T], then press 0, 1, 2, or 3 at the prompt to meet
your requirements.  For example, press 1 to make [Enter] send a carriage return
and a line feed, instead of just a carriage return.
